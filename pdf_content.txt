Uganda's Vision 2040 sets a transformative ambition: from a predominantly low-income economy to a competitive
upper-middle-income country within 30 years, growing GDP from USD 17 billion (2010) to USD 580.5 billion by 2040. Halfway
through this journey, the government has acknowledged that the pace of growth at a 7% average is insufficient. The 10-Fold
Growth Strategy was therefore developed under H.E. the President's guidance to accelerate this transformation — requiring
the economy to grow ten times (from USD 60bn to USD 600bn) within 15 years. The Fourth National Development Plan
(NDPIV) is the first plan to operationalise this strategy, targeting a doubling of GDP to USD 158 billion by FY 2029/30.

3. GDP Trajectory: Historical, Baseline & 10-Fold Target
Uganda GDP from USD 17bn (2010) → USD 60bn (2025) → USD 600bn (2040 target)
 Figure 2: Uganda GDP trajectory showing historical growth (navy), 7% baseline projection (grey, dashed), and the 10-Fold Growth Strategy
 target path (green). NDPIV aims to double GDP to USD 158bn by FY2029/30 as the first milestone.
The chart presents three scenarios for Uganda's GDP growth trajectory between 2010 and 2040:
 Historical (navy line): Uganda grew from USD 17bn in 2010 to approximately USD 60bn in 2025, averaging around 7%
per year — solid but insufficient for Vision 2040.
 Baseline 7% path (grey dashed): If Uganda continues at its historical average rate, GDP reaches approximately USD
166bn by 2040 — far short of the USD 580.5bn Vision 2040 target.
 10-Fold Growth Strategy (green): Requires accelerating to double-digit growth, reaching USD 158bn by 2030 (NDPIV
milestone), USD 350bn by 2035, and USD 600bn by 2040.
The gap between the baseline and the 10-fold path represents the 'implementation gap' that NDPIV's structural reforms, ATMS
investments, and policy consensus aim to close. This is explicitly described in the NPA presentation as requiring 'business
unusual' — a fundamental shift in how government plans and delivers.

5. NDP Timeline: Six 5-Year Plans (2010–2040)
Uganda's Comprehensive Integrated National Development Planning Framework — three phases
 Figure 4: Gantt-style NDP timeline across three governance phases (1986–2040). NDPI–III are complete; NDPIV is running. The 10-Fold
 Growth Strategy period spans the last 15 years (2025–2040).
Uganda's development planning has evolved through three distinct phases:
 Phase 1 — Fundamental Economic Reforms (1986–1996): Focused on economic recovery, structural reforms and
restoring macroeconomic stability following decades of conflict. Significant recovery achieved, though challenges of low
agricultural productivity and unemployment persisted.
 Phase 2 — Poverty Eradication Action Plan / PEAP (1997–2009): Expanded public services and focused on poverty
reduction. Poverty fell from around 56% (late 1980s) to 24.5% by 2010. However, the economy remained less competitive
due to under-developed infrastructure and low human capital.
 Phase 3 — Growth and Socio-Economic Transformation (2010–present): Implementation of six 5-year NDPs under
the Comprehensive National Development Planning Framework (CNDPF). NDPI, II, and III built the infrastructure and
human capital foundation. NDPIV now delivers the 10-Fold Strategy.
The 10-Fold Growth Strategy period covers the final 15 years of Vision 2040 (2025–2040), encompassing NDPIV, NDP V, and
NDP VI. NDPIV alone is expected to double the economy.

formalisation.

15. Economic Structure Transformation — GDP Composition
Agriculture must shrink from 53% to 10% of GDP; Industry must triple from 9.5% to 31.4%
 Figure 14: GDP sector composition — 2010 baseline vs 2040 target. The structural transformation requires agriculture's dominance to be
 replaced by industry and services, mirroring the development paths of East Asian economies.
One of the most fundamental changes required by Vision 2040 is a transformation in the structure of Uganda's economy. In
2010, agriculture dominated GDP at 53.1% — typical of a least-developed, agrarian economy. By 2040, the plan requires a
near-complete structural reversal:

This structural transformation is not automatic — it requires deliberate policy interventions: agro-industrialisation to shift

where agriculture's GDP share fell from majority to single digits over 30–40 years of deliberate industrialisation.

With approximately 65.6% of Uganda's labour force in agriculture in 2010 — most at subsistence level — the labour force
transformation is the human face of the 10-Fold Growth Strategy. As agro-industrialisation and manufacturing grow, workers
must transition from subsistence farming to formal employment in industry and services. This requires: vocational training
aligned to industrial needs; the National Human Resource Development Plan (NHRDP) that accompanies NDPIV; centralised
university admission to prioritise skills in demand; and a National Talent Register to track and deploy Uganda's skilled
workforce. NDPIV's target of creating 885,000 formal jobs per year is the quantified expression of this labour transition
imperative.
